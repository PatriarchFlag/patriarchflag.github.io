The Wage Gap is Biblical

The wage gap based on biological sex is a myth in America. But if it weren't, it would still be biblical.

The Myth in America

The "gender wage gap myth" refers to the misconception that women make 80-84 cents for doing the exact same job as men. While unadjusted median earnings show this gap, analyzing data by identical job, hours worked, education, and tenure reveals the "controlled" gap shrinks to about 98-99 cents on the dollar.

The remaining 1-2 cent difference and the broader 16-20 cent raw average gap are driven by a variety of distinct factors:

  • Occupational Sorting: Men and women often pursue different fields. Women are more likely to cluster in lower-paying but socially vital industries (like caregiving and education), while men heavily dominate higher-paying STEM and trade fields.
  • Career Interruptions: Women are statistically more likely to leave the workforce or reduce hours to care for children or aging family members, which impacts long-term earnings and career progression.
  • Hours Worked: When looking at total full-time work, men are statistically more likely to work longer hours or take on demanding, "always-on" schedules that are rewarded with higher compensation.
  • Negotiation and Experience: Disparities can also reflect differences in continuous, uninterrupted job tenure and the frequency of salary negotiations.

Men and Women have different economic worth

Leviticus 27 gives instructions on how to valuate things that someone wanted to thank God for, symbolically sacrifice to God, or in some way offered up as part of a religious ceremony. People, livestock, houses, lands, fields, and produce were all valued differently.

Of particular interest is the section where Leviticus 27:1-8 established different values for men and women.

Rules about Valuations

1 Then the LORD said to Moses, 2 “Speak to the Israelites and say to them, ‘When someone makes a special vow to the LORD involving the value of persons, 3 if the valuation concerns a male from twenty to sixty years of age, then your valuation shall be fifty shekels of silver, according to the sanctuary shekel. 4 Or if it is a female, then your valuation shall be thirty shekels. 5 And if the person is from five to twenty years of age, then your valuation for the male shall be twenty shekels, and for the female ten shekels. 6 Now if the person is from one month to five years of age, then your valuation for the male shall be five shekels of silver, and for the female three shekels of silver. 7 And if the person is sixty years of age or older, then your valuation shall be fifteen shekels for the male and ten shekels for the female. 8 But if the one making the vow is too poor to pay the valuation, he is to present the person before the priest, who shall set the value according to what the one making the vow can afford.

Especially at the time, with so much work being agricultural or related to livestock, men's physical strength would have made them much more productive in the workforce. Women's work would have been more domestic and even agricultural labor like Ruth gleaning in the fields or Rebekah watering the camels would have been relatively lighter.

The Bible wisely recognizes this reality. It is a reality with God's own stamp of approval. Both men and women are valuable, but they are not equally valuable in all the same domains of life. How many children can a man bear? A woman is much more valuable for childbearing. In fact, she is essential, since a man cannot be pregnant. A grave problem in our society is that it tells women they are more valuable economically than they are as wives and mothers.

Of course, the employer in the Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard was willing to be generous to his workers, giving those who worked only the last hour the same pay as those who worked all day. But that generosity was a gift, not something to be demanded.

Women: Your true value doesn't come from the money you earn. A greater value is the purpose Adam recognized in Eve: "the mother of all living" (Genesis 3:20) and recognized by the Apostle Paul when he wrote that part of a woman's sanctification was to engage in the work of child-bearing and child-rearing (1 Timothy 2:15).
